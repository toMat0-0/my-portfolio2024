Cat-Datasets

To expand the emotional sound classification task, I turned to a larger dataset of cat vocalizations — about 400 audio files recorded under different conditions. This dataset helped test the generalizability of my models beyond dog barks.

Each audio file is stored in a structured directory and follows a consistent naming convention:

All WAV files in this dataset are labeled with the cat's breed (Maine Coon or European Shorthair), sex and neuter status, and one of three emotional contexts: brushing, waiting for food,or isolation in an unfamiliar environment. This structure made it easier to explore how different cats express emotion through vocalization.

What's in the Dataset?

The dataset includes vocalizations across three emotional contexts and two breeds. And we also split the datastes into 80% training and 20% testing sets to evaluate model performance.

1. Breed Classification

Before diving straight into emotion recognition, I wanted to first test something more structured: could machine learning distinguish between breeds based on meows?

I was still skeptical about whether FFT-transformed audio could actually provide meaningful features for modeling. Instead of starting with a more ambiguous target like “emotion,” I focused on breed — where differences are more physical and stable. If the models couldn't handle that, they’d probably struggle even more with subjective emotional tones.

So I applied the same pipeline used in the dog dataset: PCA for dimensionality reduction followed by Logistic Regression and LDA. The results were surprisingly clear — both models achieved accuracy above 90%.

As you can see from the plot on the right, the two breed are well separated, with only a small amount of overlap:

2. Back to Emotional classification

The excellent results in cat breed classification boosted my confidence in the overall modeling approach. With a larger sample size than the dog dataset, I was hopeful that emotion categorization might also yield stronger performance.

Continuing with the linear modeling strategy, the PCA + logistic regression (64.4%), performed better than LDA(52.9%). In comparison, earlier attempts on the smaller dog dataset yielded accuracies between 50–60%, suggesting only minor gains.

You can try rotating, zooming in and out of the plot on the left.

3. Nonlinear Modeling with Random Forest

As mentioned earlier, even with a larger dataset, linear models struggled to accurately classify emotional states. This led me to consider whether a nonlinear approach might better capture the complexity in the data — so I turned to a Random Forest classifier.

The results were interesting: the overall accuracy remained around 64%, close to what we have before. However, the multidimensional scaling (MDS) plot (right) revealed something more subtle — partial clustering, especially for the isolation class.

At first, I was a little disappointed that accuracy didn’t improve more. But a comment from Prof.Lubberts shifted my perspective. He pointed out that the two categories with overlapping clusters — grooming and feeding — both involve human interaction, while isolation represents the cat being alone. That contrast, which reflects a real behavioral difference, may have been what the model was picking up on. If so, the Random Forest may not have failed — it may have succeeded at recognizing other thing meaningful.

To interpret what the model learned, I examined partial dependence plots of the most important spectral features. These plots showed nonlinear threshold-like behavior—for example, the probability of classifying a sound as isolation dropped sharply past a certain spectral value. These patterns demonstrate that Random Forests can capture more complex effects than linear models, even though the gain in overall accuracy was modest.

4. Model Performance Summary

The charts summarize classification accuracy across all models tested for both cat breed and cat emotion. Breed classification achieved high accuracy with both logistic regression and LDA, reaching 94.3%.

Emotion classification was more challenging. While PCA + LDA achieved the best performance at 65.5%, other models such as Random Forest and logistic regression performed similarly. Raw-spectrum LDA performed worse, highlighting the importance of preprocessing and feature transformation.
